# The "Quick Question" That Tells Me Everything

Tuesday, 2pm. Discovery call with a SaaS founder.

"Quick question - can you just look at our product and tell us what's wrong?"

I already know how this ends.

Not because I'm psychic. Because what you ask in the first 30 seconds tells me whether you've diagnosed your problem correctly, or whether you're about to hire the wrong person to fix the wrong thing.

(Usually the second one.)

I get five questions most often. Here's what they actually mean.

---

## 1. "Can you just look at our product and tell us what's wrong?"

Last month: founder asks this. I say "Describe the problem in one sentence."

Long pause. Then: "Well, it's not really one thing. It's more like... the whole experience is kind of..."

Right. Not one thing. That's the actual problem.

You can't point to a specific broken flow because everything's a bit broken. You have zero designers, or one junior designer drowning, or developers making UI decisions between pull requests.

What you're really asking: Can you own our product direction because nobody here thinks about design holistically and we're not sure how to admit that?

This is [product design](https://dnsk.work/services/product-design) - someone who owns the entire design direction, not just makes individual screens prettier. Not a consultant who emails you a report. Not a freelancer who delivers mockups and disappears.

Someone who figures out what's actually broken, then works with you to fix it.

(With you. Not for you. Big difference.)

I had a founder last year who wanted me to "just look" at their dashboard. Seventeen screens. Four user types. Zero consistency. Every feature designed by whoever built it.

"Just looking" took three months and cost them a product redesign.

That wasn't a quick look. That was embedded product leadership they didn't know they needed.

---

## 2. "We need someone who can do both the UX and the UI - is that you?"

I talked to a PM last week who'd spent $80K in one year hiring three different people:

Designer #1: Visual designer. Made everything beautiful. Users still confused.  
Designer #2: UX researcher. Delivered an 80-page research report. Nothing shipped.  
Designer #3: Frontend developer. Built everything. Looked like 2003 had a baby with Excel.

"So... can one person actually do both?"

Yes. But you've been hiring the wrong split.

UX without UI gets you wireframes developers can't build. UI without UX gets you gorgeous screens nobody understands how to use. What you need is [UX/UI design](https://dnsk.work/services/ux-ui-design) - someone who thinks through the experience AND executes the interface.

Most people do one well. Some do both. You've been hiring incomplete people for a complete job.

The PM asked: "Why don't portfolios show this?"

Because portfolios show pretty pictures, not thinking. You're hiring based on the wrong evidence.

(Also because design portfolios are theater. But that's a different rant.)

---

## 3. "Our product looks fine, but users keep getting stuck - can you help?"

This is my favorite question. Because you've already diagnosed it correctly.

You don't have an aesthetic problem. You have a [UX design](https://dnsk.work/services/ux-design) problem.

Your UI probably looks professional. Someone spent time on spacing, typography, color systems. Maybe even built a design system. But users are confused anyway.

I see this constantly:
- Support answering the same question 47 times a week
- Onboarding completion rate stuck at 28%
- People sign up, click around for 90 seconds, never come back

The interface looks good in screenshots. The experience is broken in practice.

Last month: looked at a product that had spent $40K on UI polish. Gorgeous design system. Every component pixel-perfect.

Users kept getting stuck at step 3 of onboarding. Support tickets piling up. "How do I [incredibly basic thing]?"

Turns out: the button for step 3 was labeled "Configure workspace settings." 

Nobody clicks that. Everyone searches for "add team member."

Beautiful button. Wrong words. Classic UX problem hiding behind pretty UI.

Common attempted fix: "Can we just add tooltips to explain it?"

No. Tooltips are admissions of defeat dressed up as helpful features. If your button needs a tooltip to explain what it does, your button is lying to users.

Fix the button. Change "Configure workspace settings" to "Add team members."

(Took 30 seconds. Saved three months of support tickets.)

---

## 4. "We're building a SaaS product - do you do that kind of work?"

Translation: We hired someone from consumer apps and it was expensive.

Last year: team hired a designer with a stunning portfolio. Previous work at a dating app and a fitness tracker. Beautiful, delightful, Instagram-worthy work.

Completely wrong for their B2B analytics product.

Three months in: designer kept adding playful animations, delightful empty states, gamification elements. "We should make it more engaging!"

Their users: accountants trying to pull quarterly reports at 4:45pm on Friday before month-end close. With their CFO waiting.

Users don't want engagement. They want efficiency. They want to export the report and close the damn tab.

Consumer apps optimize for delight and daily usage. SaaS products optimize for "get work done fast and leave." Your users aren't opening your product for fun. They're trying to finish a task under pressure, often with their boss watching.

[SaaS product design](https://dnsk.work/services/saas-product-design) means understanding your user's success metric is "finished and left" - not "spent 30 minutes exploring features."

The designer kept asking: "But how do we increase engagement?"

You don't. You decrease friction. Different problem entirely.

Admin permissions aren't delightful. Role-based access isn't playful. Multi-user approval workflows with different permission levels aren't viral moments.

Wrong designer = expensive three-month lesson about B2B psychology.

---

## 5. "Our marketing site doesn't match our product at all - can you fix that?"

Founder calls last month. Marketing site promises: "Intuitive dashboard you'll master in minutes."

I log into their product.

Seventeen menu items across the top. Four nested dropdown menus. Three different "Settings" sections in different places. Tabs inside modals inside slideover panels.

"Where's the intuitive part?"

"Oh, users get used to it eventually. After the training."

Right. The training you don't mention on the marketing site that promises "intuitive" and "master in minutes."

This happens so often I've stopped being surprised:

Marketing site: Beautiful, aspirational, "Transform your business in days"  
Product reality: Dashboard that looks like tax software from 2007  
User reaction: "Wait... is this the same product?"

Or worse: Marketing promises features the product doesn't have yet. Engineering built something slightly different. Marketing never updated the site. Sales is selling version A. Product team shipped version B. Users signed up expecting version A.

Everyone's confused except marketing, who wrote the copy six months ago and moved on to growth campaigns.

What you need: [SaaS website design](https://dnsk.work/services/saas-website-design) that doesn't lie about what your product actually is.

Match your product's interface language. Set realistic expectations. Use the same terminology your product uses. Echo your product's visual design.

(Or at least stop actively contradicting it.)

The website's job isn't to trick people into signing up. It's to prepare them for the product they're about to use.

## The Question That Wastes Everyone's Time

"Can you just look at it and tell us what to do?"

Last year: founder asked this. I spent two hours auditing their product. Wrote detailed recommendations. Specific problems. Tactical fixes. Sent it over.

Radio silence.

Six months later: "Hey! We're finally ready to start on those recommendations."

Cool story. Here's what happened in those six months:

Your product got worse. Your team shipped five new features on top of the broken foundation. Your competitors shipped three times. Your users got more confused. Every recommendation I gave you is now outdated.

(Also: you're not "ready." You're desperate. There's a difference.)

The recommendations you asked for six months ago assumed you'd fix the foundation first. Instead you built a second floor on quicksand. Now the fixes are 3x more complex.

If you want a report to file away, hire a consultant. If you want to fix the actual problem, that requires working together. Partnership, not homework.

I don't write reports anymore.

---

## What You Should Actually Ask

Don't ask: "Can you help with our design?"

Too vague. I have no idea what you need.

Ask: "We have [specific problem]. We think we need [specific thing]. Is that what you do?"

Real examples from calls last month:

"Our product has no design leadership. Developers make UI decisions. We think we need embedded product design."

"Users get lost in our onboarding flow. We see where they drop off but not why. Is this a UX problem you can solve?"

"Our marketing site promises intuitive but our actual product confuses everyone. We need these to match. Do you do that?"

One sentence. Specific problem. Clear ask.

If you can describe your problem in one sentence, I can tell you in 30 seconds whether we're a fit.

If you can't describe it in one sentence, you don't have a design problem. You have three problems wearing a trench coat pretending to be one problem. We need to unpack those first before anyone designs anything.

---

The question you ask tells me everything about whether you know what you're trying to fix.

Most people don't. That's fine. But let's figure that out in the first call, not three months into a project.

Ask a better question.
